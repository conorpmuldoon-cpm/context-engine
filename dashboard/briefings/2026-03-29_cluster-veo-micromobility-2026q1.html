Executive Summary

The Veo micromobility program has emerged as a centerpiece of Mayor Owens' transportation equity agenda and achieved substantial operational success in 2025, but faces persistent council scrutiny around safety protocols, rider equity, and contract governance. The 2025 season generated 648,000 rides exceeding 1 million miles with 24% year-over-year growth (CTX-MANUAL-2026-00001, CTX-WEB-2026-00005), and the program was successfully relaunched at full capacity on March 9, 2026 following winter suspension. However, a January 2026 council decision to hold the contract extension for committee review signals deep institutional skepticism. The February 4 Public Transportation Committee hearing revealed substantive policy disagreements: council members pressed Veo representatives on mid-ride motor cutoffs causing safety incidents, underage rider access gaps, helmet enforcement, intoxication risks, and battery management protocols (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00027 through CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00030). Veo's $250K total new investment for 2026, including $50K revenue share at 15 cents per trip and $130K in opportunity zone rider rebates, demonstrates financial commitment to equity (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00025), but council concerns about liability, incident response, and minor protection remain unresolved. The Mayor has championed the program as aligned with Vision Zero and transportation equity goals (CTX-MANUAL-2026-00001), yet council's insistence on constituent input before approving the extension reflects constituent complaints and neighborhood safety concerns that operate in parallel with micromobility expansion discussions (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00051).

Recent Council Activity

Contract Extension Hold and Committee Review On January 7, 2026, the Common Council held Veo's contract extension request pending committee review, directing Veo representatives to present performance statistics and answer constituent questions (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00015). Council President Eric Annis sought realignment of contract dates from the February procurement cycle back to the January/calendar year. The hold remained in effect through January 26 (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00108) with a public hearing scheduled for February 4 via the Public Transportation Committee.

February 4 Public Transportation Committee Hearing Chair Nave convened the Public Transportation Committee on February 4 with Veo representatives Jeffrey Hoover, Paul Kalabufo, and Reed Neil presenting 2025 results and 2026 operational improvements (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00025). The hearing spanned four substantive record segments:

  • Revenue Share & Investment (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00025, CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00026): Veo's 15-cent-per-trip revenue share yielded approximately $50,000 to the city in 2025 (excluding Access program and opportunity zone trips). The company committed $250K in total new investment for 2026, including $130K in opportunity zone rider rebates—a significant equity program targeting low-income neighborhoods. Automated tipover detection technology reduced response time from 16 hours to 4 hours 45 minutes despite a 40% increase in tipped vehicles per day (140 more/day).
  • Accessibility & Safety Tools (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00027): Veo presented two new vehicle types: a three-wheeled trike designed for riders with mobility limitations (with storage for oxygen tanks, walkers, larger bags) and a cargo bike targeting food desert areas, specifically zip code 13205. Centro transit integration was announced, offering riders free/discounted trips when connecting to Centro bus stops. Council pressed heavily on minor rider access, intoxication risks, and helmet enforcement gaps. Veo argued that mandatory ID verification creates equity barriers for unbanked and undocumented riders and could drive minors to unsafe unregulated e-bikes, but California-style state-mandated verification was presented as an alternative (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00029).
  • Battery & Motor Safety (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00028): Council questioned practical operations, including battery range (45 miles full charge, swap every 10 rides), low-funds notification (verbal alert from bike, $20 pre-authorization to prevent mid-ride cutoff), and mid-ride motor cutoffs. A council member reported witnessing a near-accident when a bike motor cut off mid-ride. Veo described damage liability processes but council expressed concern about incident documentation and constituent complaints.
  • Fleet Maintenance & Fire Safety (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00030): Paul Kalabufo detailed a four-tier maintenance system: self-diagnostic error mode (removes vehicle from app), customer phone reports, systematic weekly rotation (10 scooters/week through full fleet), and seasonal full-fleet overhaul. All vehicles include self-sealing tires and brake failure detection. Lithium battery fire safety protocols were discussed, though specific details on rider violation enforcement were not fully resolved.

No final vote was recorded in the February 4 session; council signaled the need for further deliberation on safety protocols and equity trade-offs.
