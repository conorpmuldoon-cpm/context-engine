Executive Summary

Code Enforcement Reform has shifted from a budget crisis in 2025 to operational momentum in 2026. The Department of Code Enforcement secured $1M in additional funding (approved May 2025) to hire two inspectors and contract with six third-party plan review firms. The reform strategy is showing measurable results: permit turnaround has improved dramatically (projects now assigned same-day vs. sitting in queue for weeks), and the third-party firms are up for renewal in 2026 with council backing.

However, the reform sits at a crossroads. The core enforcement backlog—evidenced by high-profile failures (Nob Hill Apartments $340K in fines, Skyline/Metropolitan receiver action, Maria Regina fire/demolition dispute)—reveals systemic capacity gaps that no single budget injection has fully resolved. The NYS Uniform Building Code update remains stalled pending state guidance (held since January 2026), creating regulatory uncertainty. Incoming Mayor Sharon Owens has signaled continuity on code enforcement as part of broader neighborhood development strategy, but the intensity and resources available under a new administration remain unclear.

Key political players: Jake Dishaw (Deputy Commissioner of Code Enforcement) is actively championing third-party plan review renewal and entertainment licensing reform. Councilor Marty Nave has emerged as a skeptic of enforcement timeliness and transparency (Maria Regina dispute, CTX-NEWS-2025-00354). Common Council broadly supports permitting modernization but balked at unrelated budget cuts to Code Enforcement in 2025.
