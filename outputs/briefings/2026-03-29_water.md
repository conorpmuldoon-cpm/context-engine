## Executive Summary

The Department of Water faces a critical credibility and execution challenge centered on lead service line remediation. The city has now publicly admitted it will take a decade or longer to replace all lead pipes—doubling the originally promised five-year timeline—after replacing only 700 of 3,000 targeted pipes in 2025 (CTX-NEWS-2025-00281). While the department has achieved three consecutive quarters of EPA lead compliance (testing at 12 ppb versus the 15 ppb action level), community trust is eroding due to perceived slow progress, procurement missteps, and a gap between public commitments and operational capacity.

The department operates under federal and state regulatory pressure. The EPA has mandated full service line replacement (main to meter) by November 2027, covering 2,900 identified homes in the current phase (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00053). Funding includes a $2M one-time federal grant for lead inventory and $5M bonded in 2025 (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00057), but contractor capacity and homeowner coordination remain bottlenecks. A recent vendor transition from 120 Water (emergency hire) to Trupanti reflects procurement governance concerns and Council skepticism about past selection decisions (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00054).

Community organizations and advocacy coalitions are escalating pressure. Approximately 600 children per year are poisoned by lead in Onondaga County, with water exposure adding compounding risk (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00056). Organizations like Families for Lead Freedom continue public testimony demanding faster replacement and better equity prioritization. The department's educational outreach has improved (CTX-WEB-2026-00033), but operational execution remains the primary vulnerability.

## Recent Council Activity

**Lead Remediation Pace & Mandate Compliance**

The Common Council Public Works Committee has held multiple detailed hearings on lead remediation strategy. Commissioner Robert Brandt reported the city is already conducting full service line replacements (main to meter) ahead of the November 2027 EPA mandate, positioning the city as proactive rather than reactive (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00053). However, Council member Dr. Chol Majok challenged the replacement pace relative to the federal mandate, with the commissioner defending the "proactive full replacement" approach over minimum EPA public-side-only requirements (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00052).

The core tension: the city's 2,900-home replacement map targets homes where public-side replacements occurred in 2005-2007 but private-side lead remains (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00053). Council questioned whether this strategy adequately prioritizes homes with children, pushing the department toward equity-based scoring for child population density (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00055).

**Vendor Transition & Procurement Governance**

Council scrutiny of the 120 Water-to-Trupanti vendor transition reveals governance concerns. The city was "overcharged" by 120 Water, an emergency hire recommended by Buffalo (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00054). Trupanti won the RFP after reference checks and serves St. Paul, Minneapolis, and most of Texas; it is the vendor's first New York State client. Council questioned whether 120 Water selection was justified and why community organizations were excluded from procurement discussions (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00054).

**Funding & Contractor Strategy**

Commissioner outlined phased contractor strategy: broke lead replacement contracts into smaller packages to attract more bidders. Phase 1 awarded to 2 contractors for 2,900 private-side-only completions (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00058). Phase 2 planned to draw additional contractors and anticipate "fierce nationwide competition" for skilled labor (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00058).

Funding sources: $2M one-time federal grant (lead service line inventory), $5M bonded in 2025, $48K/year annual testing contract with Trupanti (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00057). Council pressed on replacement pace relative to 14,000+ known lead lines and identified "bureaucratic barriers to replacement" as a key constraint (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00057).

**Infrastructure & Leak Detection**

Council approved extension of American Leak Detection contract for water distribution system monitoring using specialized sensors on conduits, services, and water mains. Council requested cost-benefit analysis quantifying water savings from early leak detection—indicating demand for ROI data on infrastructure programs (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00126).

Water Department employee lease renewal at 20 West Genesee Street approved with 5% rate increase (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00071).
