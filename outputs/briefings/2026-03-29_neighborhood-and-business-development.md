## Executive Summary

The Department of Neighborhood and Business Development is managing a complex portfolio of housing redevelopment, code enforcement modernization, and economic development initiatives amid significant infrastructure and equity pressures. The most consequential active project is Parkside Commons, a 393-unit mixed-income redevelopment generating intense council scrutiny over community engagement, green space preservation, and financial transparency (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00043, CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00045). Simultaneously, the city is navigating a major code enforcement operational overhaul—third-party plan review has reduced permit processing times dramatically, but competitive bidding waivers for high-value contracts are drawing equity challenges from Councilor Caldwell (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00088, CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00077). The Micron Technology economic development strategy is reshaping land use priorities (ShoppingTown pivot toward R&D, LaFayette Hills housing delays), while federal grant cancellations—the Trump administration eliminated Syracuse's $30 million Neighborhood Access and Equity Program award for I-81 corridor work—have forced the city to pursue state replacement funding (CTX-NEWS-2025-00252, CTX-NEWS-2025-00274). Commissioner Michael Collins and newly appointed Code Enforcement Director Ryan Shiel lead department operations under continued mayoral priority alignment with climate action (Community Solar program) and affordable housing delivery.

## Recent Council Activity

**Parkside Commons Redevelopment (Ongoing Scrutiny)**

The council is conducting extended oversight of the Parkside Commons Phase 1 project (BFC Partners/SAEVI joint venture, 393 units across west and east campuses). Key tensions:

- **Community Engagement:** Council demanded door-to-door engagement with both tenants and neighboring homeowners, citing dissatisfaction with initial cold-weather meetings that drew minimal attendance (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00044). Council member Patrona Jones-Rowser explicitly referenced the "East Adams standard" of accountability and warned developers: "the city has failed that corridor for a long time" with "decades and decades of broken housing promises" (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00047).

- **Green Space & Site Plan:** Council pressed developers on concentrating 393 units and pressed for transparency on east campus demolition plans (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00043, CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00045). Commissioner Collins acknowledged east campus is "physically infeasible for safe operation" due to poor mid-century design (camera-height ceilings enabling vandalism), but council wants concrete timeline and financial modeling for replacement buildings.

- **Financial Transparency:** Council demanded full disclosure of $220M capital stack and shelter rent PILOT agreement terms (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00045). Developers present 15-year shelter rent agreement for city approval (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00041).

**Code Enforcement & Permitting Reform**

- **Third-Party Plan Review:** Jake Dishaw (Deputy Commissioner) reported dramatic operational improvement—projects now assigned same day vs. sitting in queue for weeks. Council advocated for increased budget for third-party plan review firms, framing it as ROI-positive (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00088).

- **Entertainment Licensing Reform:** Item 52 passed, creating two license types based on 350-person crowd threshold. Large venues need per-event licenses for emergency response; small venues get annual permits. Sentiment: positive (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00075).

- **Building Code Hold:** NYS Uniform Fire Prevention and Building Code update (Item 27) held pending rescheduled state meeting. Technical housing corrections advanced (Items 28-29) (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00022).

**Land Bank & Foreclosure Management**
