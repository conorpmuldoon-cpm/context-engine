## Executive Summary

Syracuse is navigating a critical fiscal inflection point. The FY2025 independent audit (Bonadio Group, March 2026) delivered a clean opinion but surfaced persistent structural vulnerabilities: a material weakness in capital projects tracking (repeat finding), $710M in unfunded retiree health liability, and multiple grant management delays costing the city reimbursement timing. Simultaneously, the city is confronting the "ARPA cliff"—$80M+ in federal pandemic relief funds is now exhausted, creating a budget sustainability challenge that prompted Council to request an independent audit of Police and Fire department budgets (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00069). On the positive side, the State Senate proposed $30M in additional aid to Syracuse (double the Governor's executive budget), signaling legislative backing (CTX-WEB-2026-00003, CTX-WEB-2026-00018). However, revenue shortfalls are real: property tax came in $5M under budget, sales tax declined (recession indicator), and competitive bid waiver practices drew explicit Council scrutiny during the March 16 Finance Committee deep-dive. The city's $20M budgetary surplus masks underlying structural challenges; Council is now demanding transparency on fund balance deployment strategy and debt capacity limits.

## Budget Context

**ARPA Cliff & Structural Deficit**
The city received $80M+ in American Rescue Plan Act funds over 2021–2025. These are now "essentially exhausted," forcing a structural rebalancing. The FY2025 $20M surplus was driven largely by ~$8M in remaining ARPA carryover and expenditure savings from unfilled positions. Without ARPA, the city faces a structural revenue gap estimated at $8–15M annually (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00148).

**Retiree Health Liability**
The FY2025 audit identified $710M in unfunded retiree health benefits (Pension Obligation Bond (POB) liability). This is a long-term structural obligation that constrains debt capacity and limits fund balance deployment (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00147).

**Capital Projects Tracking Failure**
The material weakness in capital projects tracking—a repeat finding from prior audits—stems from manual (non-system) tracking of revenue recognition and receivables on grants. This caused misstatements in FY2025 and contributed to $6M in delayed grant reimbursements (e.g., DOT/CHIPS: $36M spent, only $30M claimed). The auditors flagged this as a controls deficiency requiring immediate remediation (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00149).

**State Aid Windfall**
The proposed $30M in additional NYS aid (announced March 2026) would provide meaningful relief if enacted. This would offset approximately 2 years of the projected structural deficit and signals state legislative support for Syracuse's recovery (CTX-WEB-2026-00003).

**Fiscal Adviser & Debt Issuance**
The city renewed its fiscal adviser contract (Bonadio Group, local firm at 250 South Clinton St) for government bond and short-term debt issuance at ~$90K annually ($25K June general offering, $8–10K for short-term debt). This is a routine procurement but reflects ongoing capital financing needs (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00100).
