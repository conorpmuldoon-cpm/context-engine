## Executive Summary

Syracuse is experiencing acute political friction over homelessness service siting and infrastructure deployment. The cluster centers on three overlapping crises: (1) a chain-link fence installed by the city along Dickerson Street behind the Syracuse Rescue Mission in June 2025 without advance notice; (2) a paused county-city proposal for a daytime drop-in warming/cooling center on West Onondaga Street following neighborhood opposition; and (3) broader questions about equitable service distribution and community consultation.

The political landscape is dominated by **constituent pressure** from business owners and residents in the West Onondaga/Dickerson Street corridor, coupled with **opposition from key stakeholders**—notably the Executive Director of the Rescue Mission itself. The fence was framed by city officials as a public safety measure to reduce drug activity and violence, but unhoused residents and advocacy groups have characterized it as containment/displacement rather than solutions. A county-funded drop-in center ($1.5M+ in opioid settlement proceeds and state funding) was paused in July 2025 after a neighborhood petition and direct business community pressure. No major new homelessness services have launched since the pause; the policy environment remains polarized.

**Key players:** Onondaga County Department of Health (leading drop-in center planning), Syracuse Rescue Mission leadership (public opposition to fence), West Onondaga Street business owners and residents (organized opposition), Department of Neighborhood and Community Services, unhoused community and advocacy organizations. The administration has not issued a comprehensive homelessness strategy statement; initiatives remain siloed and reactive.
