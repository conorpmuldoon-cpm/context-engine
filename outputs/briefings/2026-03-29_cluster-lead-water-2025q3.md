## Executive Summary

Syracuse faces a complex and politically volatile lead water crisis marked by a sharp disconnect between technical compliance metrics and community trust. The Department of Water has achieved three consecutive EPA sampling cycles below the 15 ppb action level (most recent: 12 ppb at 90th percentile, CTX-WEB-2026-00010), positioning the city ahead of the stricter 2027 EPA mandate (10 ppb). However, this regulatory success is being actively contradicted by organized advocacy groups—including Families for Lead Freedom Now, EarthJustice, and NRDC—who filed an EPA petition (CTX-NEWS-2025-00102) and escalated demands for emergency federal intervention (CTX-NEWS-2025-00096) based on 2024 sampling showing lead levels 7x EPA limits.

The core operational failure centers on lead service line replacement pace. Commissioner Robert Brandt publicly acknowledged in December 2025 that the city will require a decade or longer to replace all lead pipes instead of the promised five years, having replaced only 700 of 3,000 targeted pipes in 2025 (CTX-NEWS-2025-00281). The city now projects 1,700 replacements for 2026 against approximately 14,000 known lead service lines citywide (CTX-NEWS-2025-00197). Funding is fragmented: $2M federal grant for inventory, $5M bonded in 2025 for continued replacements beyond pump station projects (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00057), with additional capital commitments tied to infrastructure projects like the Mosley Pump Station replacement beginning September 2, 2025 (CTX-WEB-2026-00040).

Political pressure is intensifying across three fronts: federal EPA escalation (petition filed, emergency powers requested), council-level scrutiny (Chair Dr. Chol Majok framing lead as a leadership accountability issue, CTX-NEWS-2025-00016), and sustained community organizing demanding transparency and accelerated replacement. Water Commissioner Brandt's tenure—formalized in permanent appointment October 2024 (CTX-WEB-2026-00077)—now carries direct accountability for both meeting the 2027 EPA mandate and rebuilding community credibility eroded by disputed 2024 sampling results.

## Recent Council Activity

**Lead Water Sampling & Service Line Replacement Strategy (January 20, 2026)**

Chair Dr. Chol Majok opened a Public Works Committee meeting focused on testing compliance and replacement timelines (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00052). Commissioner reported third consecutive testing cycle under EPA 15 ppb action level for a 100-home sampling program. The city is transitioning testing vendors from 120 Water to Trupanti (RFP winner, serves St. Paul and Minneapolis), reflecting prior community opposition to 120 Water and concerns about procurement oversight.

Commissioner presented a 2,900-home replacement map targeting homes where public-side infrastructure was replaced 2005-2007 but private-side lead remains. The EPA mandate effective November 2027 requires full service line replacement (main to meter); the city is already proactively performing full replacements beyond the minimum mandate (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00053). Council pressed Commissioner on replacement pace relative to the 14,000+ known lead lines and the 2027 deadline.

Funding breakdown presented: $2M one-time federal grant for lead service line inventory, $5M bonded in 2025 to continue replacements beyond pump station projects (added Westmoreland from Genesee to Euclid and other areas), $48K/year for testing contract with Trupanti (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00057). Council flagged bureaucratic barriers (contractor capacity constraints, property owner coordination challenges) as constraints on acceleration.

**Equity & Prioritization Debate (January 20, 2026)**

Council pushed for prioritizing lead service line replacements at homes with children; Commissioner explained current phase covers 2,900 private-side completions via grant while citywide plan uses equity scoring for child population density (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00055). Separate debate on filter distribution: WIC collaboration mentioned, Brita Elite filters being distributed to households with pregnant women and children under six (CTX-WEB-2026-00070), though council questioned whether this is adequate long-term public health strategy versus full replacement.

**Contractor Strategy & Community Communication (January 20, 2026)**

Commissioner detailed proactive contractor strategy: broke contracts into smaller packages to attract more bidders. Five contractors attended pre-bid conference, two awarded for Phase 1 (private-side-only, 2,900 homes). Phase 2 planned to draw additional contractors to overcome nationwide competition for specialized contractors (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00058). Chair Majok committed to coordinating a collective community-department meeting to accelerate replacement and improve communication.

**Capital Bond Items (February 4, 2026)**

DPW First Deputy Commissioner presented multiple capital bond items including fiber optic trunk replacement and city-owned building improvements at $800,000 (council questioned lack of itemized list for building improvements). Significant praise for CHIPS reimbursement process reform. Water infrastructure items approved but without line-item detail scrutiny (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00125).

**Child Lead Poisoning Public Testimony (January 20, 2026)**

Oceana Fair (Families for Lead Freedom) testified that approximately 600 children per year are poisoned by lead in Onondaga County—equivalent to filling McKinley Brighton School (CTX-COUNCIL-2026-00056). Primary source is lead paint, but water exposure adds risk at lower levels; NY State action level is 5.5 micrograms/dL versus EPA standard. Fair's testimony underscored that regulatory compliance on tap water sampling does not address cumulative environmental lead exposure.

## Recent News Coverage

**Sampling Compliance Announcements (Positive Sentiment)**

Water Department reports continued EPA compliance: first-half 2025 testing from 121 homes with lead service lines at 9 ppb against 15 ppb action level, already meeting EPA's 2027 stricter requirement of 10 ppb (CTX-WEB-2026-00042). Second-half 2025 sampling confirmed 12 ppb at 90th percentile, third consecutive compliance period (CTX-WEB-2026-00010, CTX-WEB-2026-00025). Department launched educational materials on lead water safety in coordination with EPA and NYSDOH (CTX-WEB-2026-00033).

**Community & Advocacy Escalation (Critical Sentiment)**

Organizations filed EPA petition demanding investigation into elevated lead levels detected in summer 2024 (CTX-NEWS-2025-00102). Chief Policy Officer disputes findings, attributing prior exceedances to sampling errors; community organizations reject this explanation and demand accountability.

Guest opinion by Oceana Fair and Nayyirah Shariff called for urgent action on lead water contamination affecting 14,000+ families, citing testing showing lead levels 7x EPA action level (CTX-NEWS-2025-00197). Authors demand pipe replacement, transparency on sampling methodology disputes, and public health accountability. Tone is accusatory toward city administration.

Advocacy coalition including EarthJustice and NRDC petitioned EPA to invoke emergency powers under Safe Drinking Water Act to force Syracuse to address lead contamination, citing questionable sampling procedures from 2023-2024 (CTX-NEWS-2025-00096). Coalition garnered 1,800 resident signatures, escalating issue to federal regulatory level.

**Infrastructure Slippage (Critical Sentiment)**

Syracuse admitted in December 2025 that lead pipe replacement will take a decade or longer instead of the promised five years, having replaced only 700 of 3,000 targeted pipes in 2025 (CTX-NEWS-2025-00281). City now plans 1,700 replacements for 2026 and faces another federal violation. Water Commissioner Robert Brandt cited logistical challenges as the cause. Council member scrutiny and media coverage framed this as a leadership accountability issue.

**Lead Paint Ordinance Audit (Critical Sentiment)**

Families for Lead Freedom Now and EarthJustice found that Syracuse's 2022 lead paint ordinance, despite generating 7,000+ citations, is not reducing childhood lead poisoning cases (CTX-NEWS-2025-00063). Report identified enforcement gaps: repeated citations on same properties without follow-up remediation. Signal of broader systemic failure in multi-vector lead exposure mitigation.

**County-Level Coordination (Mixed Sentiment)**

Onondaga County hosted lead safety events during National Lead Poisoning Prevention Week (October 19-25, 2025) targeting families, landlords, and contractors (CTX-NEWS-2025-00244). County Executive and Legislator McCarron directly championing lead remediation, with free filter distribution and public information sessions. Parallel county initiative to address lead paint remediation reported progress: only 1% of 541 tested preschoolers showed elevated blood lead levels, 72% of 8,000+ code violations addressed (CTX-NEWS-2025-00086). However, county also conducting mandatory EPA-mandated inventory of 106,000 water service lines with 48,000 customers still unreported (CTX-NEWS-2025-00394).

**Budget Hearing Crisis Framing (Critical Sentiment)**

April 2025 budget hearing before Common Council highlighted drinking water safety crisis marked by high lead levels (among worst in the nation per NRDC), low water pressure from aging pipes, and staffing shortage of 16 employees (CTX-NEWS-2025-00016). Council member Majok framed lead contamination as a leadership problem requiring accountability.
