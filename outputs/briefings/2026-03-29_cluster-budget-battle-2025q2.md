## Executive Summary

Syracuse experienced a severe executive-legislative budget conflict in spring 2025 that resulted in the first-ever veto override of a sitting mayor. Mayor Ben Walsh proposed a $348.4M FY2026 budget on April 10, 2025, featuring a 2% property tax increase ($6.6M revenue), a 2% water rate increase, and a $27M draw from the city's $120M rainy day fund to close a projected structural deficit. The Common Council, led by Finance Chair Corey Williams and Council President Pro Tempore Pat Hogan (both mayoral candidates), rejected the tax increase and implemented across-the-board cuts totaling $16M through 82 budget amendments—processes conducted largely in closed-door meetings with external auditor Bonadio Group. Mayor Walsh issued 45 vetoes on May 19, 2025, protecting police, fire, and code enforcement spending. On May 27, 2025, the Council unanimously overrode all 45 vetoes and passed the $348M budget with cuts intact and no property tax increase, marking an unprecedented assertion of legislative budget authority. Both sides claimed vindication from third-party reviews: Moody's Ratings affirmed the city's A1 bond rating and stable outlook on June 3, 2025 (CTX-NEWS-2025-00224), citing strong 33% fund balance reserves. The budget battle exposed governance tensions over transparency, reserve depletion, departmental service capacity, and the city's fiscal sustainability trajectory.

Council ultimately implemented only $16M in cuts. The closed-door approach triggered transparency concerns, though legally defensible as a caucus session (CTX-NEWS-2025-00035).

- **May 8, 2025 (Amendment Vote):** Common Council unanimously approved 80+ budget amendments cutting $16M across all city departments (7% across-the-board reduction). - **May 12, 2025 (Transparency Critique):** Mayor Walsh and department heads publicly criticized the Council's lack of transparency during budget deliberations, citing eleventh-hour approval of 80+ amendments with no prior notice or negotiation (CTX-NEWS-2025-00032).

- **May 15, 2025 (Failed Compromise):** Mayor Walsh signaled willingness to accept $2.5M+ in budget cuts to eliminate the proposed 2% tax increase, but Councilor Pat Hogan rejected compromise and indicated predetermined intent to override mayoral budget objections (CTX-NEWS-2025-00270).

- **May 19, 2025 (Mayoral Vetoes):** Mayor Walsh issued 45 vetoes against Council amendments, protecting $13M in spending reductions across police ($3.5M), fire ($2.4M), code enforcement, and Parks departments. - **May 27, 2025 (Veto Override):** Common Council unanimously overrode all 45 of Mayor Walsh's vetoes, finalizing the $348M budget with $16M in cuts intact and no property tax increase. This represented the first unanimous veto override of a sitting Syracuse mayor (CTX-NEWS-2025-00045, CTX-NEWS-2025-00046).

## Recent News Coverage

**Initial Budget Proposal and Council Opposition (April 2025)**

Mayor Walsh's April 10 budget proposal triggered immediate and sustained council opposition. Finance Chair Corey Williams expressed concern that the budget relied too heavily on a $27M reserve draw, compounded by a 2% property tax increase, creating a "cumulative structural deficit" (CTX-NEWS-2025-00007). Councilor Pat Hogan and others opposed the tax hike despite the city's substantial $120M reserve fund surplus—which had doubled since Walsh took office—citing fiscal discipline concerns (CTX-NEWS-2025-00359).

City Auditor Alex Marion released a pre-enactment budget analysis (April 24, 2025)—the first time a Syracuse auditor reviewed a budget before passage. Marion acknowledged budget challenges while recommending revenue optimization through modernized fee structures for permitting and code enforcement (CTX-NEWS-2025-00014). A public meeting on April 29 invited resident input on the controversial budget, with constituent pressure evident against the proposed tax increase (CTX-NEWS-2025-00021). The council postponed a planned $1.8M property revaluation project (first in 30 years) due to constituent anxiety about cost-of-living impacts and tax increases (CTX-NEWS-2025-00020, CTX-NEWS-2025-00349).

**Budget Battle Escalation (May 2025)**

Media coverage intensified as the Council implemented dramatic cuts. Mayor Walsh called the cuts "draconian and dangerous," and department heads warned of service elimination—particularly in police (20-position reduction including 10 crossing guards), fire, and code enforcement. The I-Team investigation highlighted that Council members, including mayoral candidates Chol Majok and Pat Hogan, directly opposed Walsh's public safety priorities (CTX-NEWS-2025-00042). Coverage emphasized the "unprecedented" nature of the closed-door amendment process and the lack of public hearings on individual amendments despite 82 total modifications (CTX-NEWS-2025-00046).

Councilor Pat Hogan announced on May 21 that the Council would "probably" override all 45 mayoral vetoes, signaling predetermined legislative intent and triggering critical media framing of executive-legislative breakdown (CTX-NEWS-2025-00388). News outlets framed the May 27 veto override as a historic assertion of Council authority and a rebuke of mayoral fiscal conservatism (CTX-NEWS-2025-00045).

**Post-Battle Validation and Political Framing (June 2025)**

Three Democratic mayoral candidates (Deputy Mayor Sharon Owens, Councilors Pat Hogan and Chol Majok) claimed vindication from the budget outcome. Hogan and Majok framed their positions as fiscal responsibility and resistance to excessive reserve depletion, while Owens opposed the cuts as damaging to service delivery. The conflict became a campaign issue in the June primary (CTX-NEWS-2025-00025).

Moody's Ratings affirmation (June 3, 2025) of Syracuse's A1 bond rating and stable outlook—citing 33% fund balance reserves and economic growth—was claimed by both Mayor Walsh (validating his fiscal management) and the Council (validating their reserve-preservation approach). The rating agency's strong assessment undercut both sides' doomsday narratives about the other's approach (CTX-NEWS-2025-00224).

## Budget Context

**FY2026 Budget Baseline and Structural Challenges**

Mayor Walsh's proposed $348.4M budget addressed structural revenue pressures: declining sales tax revenue (downward trend across the records) and state aid reductions. The budget included commitments to infrastructure, roads/bridges (Vision Zero), police, fire, parks, workforce development, code enforcement, digital services, and housing initiatives (CTX-NEWS-2025-00002). The proposed 2% property tax increase would generate $6.6M, and the 2% water rate increase would contribute additional revenue to offset mandatory cost increases and reserve preservation (CTX-NEWS-2025-00001).

**Reserve Fund Strategy Dispute**

The city's $120M rainy day fund (unassigned fund balance) became central to the budget conflict. Mayor Walsh's $27M reserve draw raised Council concerns about fiscal sustainability. Finance Chair Williams warned of a "cumulative structural deficit" if the draw continued (CTX-NEWS-2025-00007). Councilors Hogan and Majok highlighted that the fund had doubled since Walsh took office and should preclude tax increases (CTX-NEWS-2025-00359). The Council ultimately sought to limit reserve depletion to approximately $13.5M to preserve fiscal independence (CTX-NEWS-2025-00044). Moody's validation of the city's strong financial position—citing 33% fund balance reserves—suggested both approaches had merit from a credit perspective (CTX-NEWS-2025-00224).

**External Audit Findings**

The Bonadio Group independent audit (May 9, 2025) identified 26 red flags in Walsh's budget and recommended $26M in cost reductions. The audit created institutional tension because the Council implemented only $16M in cuts, falling short of auditor recommendations and suggesting either incomplete fiscal diagnosis or deliberate Council decision to preserve more services than external analysis warranted (CTX-NEWS-2025-00024).

**Resolved Budget Outcome**

The final FY2026 budget totaled $348M with no property tax increase, $16M in departmental cuts, and a reduced (but still substantial) reserve draw. The override eliminated Walsh's proposed 2% tax increase, protecting residents from the $57/year average impact on $100K assessed properties but reducing general fund revenue (CTX-NEWS-2025-00045).
