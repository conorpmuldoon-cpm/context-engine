'We have to do better': SHA Board Chair addresses open meetings law violation

by Mary Kielar

Thu, May 8, 2025 at 3:54 PM
Updated Fri, May 9, 2025 at 12:19 PM
The SHA Board of Commissioners Chair Calvin Corriders Sr. said the agency has nothing to hide from residents or the community. (PHOTO BY CNY CENTRAL)

Syracuse, N.Y. — The Chair of the Syracuse Housing Authority Board Calvin Corriders Sr. told the I-Team that commissioners, including himself, must take responsibility for violating New York State Open Meetings Law last month.

"I could offer explanations and excuses, and I'm not going to do that. We have to do better. These are public meetings. We are and have to be a transparent organization. We have nothing to hide," he said.

On April 17, the board met for a regularly scheduled meeting, and a CNY Central crew, along with two members of the public and the city's Neighborhood and Business Development Commissioner and Deputy Commissioner were left in the lobby.

Once the public and press were invited in, it was apparent the meeting was already well underway.

The agenda obtained by the I-Team before that meeting showed the board had already gone into executive session once and voted to approve the previous meeting minutes. All of this needed to be done with the public present.

While nothing significant was decided behind closed doors during that period, Corriders said that's irrelevant.

"The point is, we're in violation. I've talked to the team about self-reporting and whatever disciplinary action that would come from the state, then we would have to own up to that and we would own up to that and whatever penalties, I'm not aware of what happens, but take accountability."

Corriders was not at the April 17 meeting in person and appeared virtually along with other people. He was there in person on Thursday and personally came out to make sure members of the public were in the room before the board meeting started.

Syracuse Common Council President Pro-Tempore Pat Hogan told the I-Team that he was surprised no one spoke out about the public being locked out of the meeting.

"It's a little puzzling that the [SHA Board] didn't say anything when the public wasn't a part of this latest meeting, I guess," said Hogan at the time.

Corriders also addressed the I-Team's reporting on a public hearing on a Friday night at SHA's central office. It lasted all of 20 minutes and only two residents showed up.

"The Housing Authority's expectation would be greater than that," said Corriders. He said, "If we have to have the conversations, what's the expectation for the number of people who attend? If we exceed that expectation, there's something to be said about that. If we didn't exceed it, then what can we do better?"
