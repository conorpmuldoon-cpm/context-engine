Syracuse City Schools spend 50% more per student yet rank near bottom in state test scores

by Lane Russell

SYRACUSE, N.Y. — The Syracuse City School District is spending nearly 50% more than the national average per student, despite some of the lowest testing scores in New York State.

This comes as the Syracuse City School District prepares for major changes in leadership, including Deputy Superintendent Pamela Odom taking over as superintendent at the end of October. The district is considering hiring a company owned by outgoing Superintendent Anthony Davis for consulting services.

Publicly available documents from the school district show the district would pay up to $125,000 if the plan to hire Davis’ company is approved. The district notes it would amount to only $6.41 per student, as taxpayers already pay higher-than-average costs for education.

Data from New York State shows that this year, Syracuse City Schools are spending $611 million to educate about 19,500 students, roughly $31,333 per student.

The national average for public school spending is a little more than $20,000 per student. That’s a $11,000 a year difference, meaning taxpayers pay 50% more than every other state in the country to educate our children.

A review of New York’s school testing data by the CNY Central I-Team shows that despite higher-than-average tax dollars being spent in the Syracuse City School District, students have some of the worst education outcomes in the entire state.

When it comes to English language arts testing, of the 658 public school districts submitting data to the state, the Syracuse City School District ranked 644th. Just one out of every five students in grades three through eight achieved a score of “proficient” in ELA. The statewide average is about 46% achieving a proficient score.

In math, the results are even worse. Of the 550 school districts that submitted data to the state, Syracuse ranks 549th. Just 18% of third through eighth graders are considered “proficient” at math according to state testing. That’s less than one out of every five students.
