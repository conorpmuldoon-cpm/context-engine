SYRACUSE, N.Y. — The NAACP Regional Director of Western Region Linda Brown-Robinson told the I-Team in a statement that the chapter has a good relationship with Syracuse Police Chief Joe Cecile.

However, she said that it has not had a working relationship with the PBA, referring to the union representing officers at the Syracuse Police Department.

The post made by PBA President Joe Moran on Sunday night.{ }

CNY Central wanted the organization's take on the controversial Sunday night Facebook post written by Syracuse Police Benevolent Association President Joe Moran.

In it, he called the city's Armory Square "chaotic," and said "gangbangers" are infiltrating the arts and entertainment area on Friday and Saturday nights. He included that there were 40 arrests and five guns recovered between this past Friday and Saturday night, but did not provide any other context.

In an interview, Brown-Robinson said, "What was crafty enough about the statement was that it did not say who the people involved were. But it had enough inference to his statement that you got the idea of what is probably people of color."

She said that it's the NAACP's duty to speak out when it sees an injustice.

Syracuse Police Chief Joe Cecile told the I-Team that he asked the Mayor and Deputy Mayor for more officers to do proactive police work to address loitering after the bars close downtown. He said the arrests were a result of that request being granted and law enforcement partners joining the effort.

President Moran told the I-Team on Tuesday that, "There are documented gang members and gangbangers infiltrating the Armory Square downtown area. That's the truth. If people are offended by it, they can get a tissue."

Brown-Robinson said the NAACP will hold judgment and further comment until additional details come in and that she would speak to branch leadership.

"The ball is in [Moran's] court to come to the NAACP. We'll come to the table. I'm pretty sure of it," said Brown-Robinson. "We can absolutely come to the table with our leadership."

Meanwhile, CNY Central asked both Onondaga County Executive Ryan McMahon and Syracuse Mayor Ben Walsh about where people could go instead of congregating downtown into the early morning hours.

"These are moment-in-time issues that we see pop up. It doesn't just mean that it's in the City of Syracuse," said McMahon. "These are mobile groups that are driving and if it's a problem in some of our suburban communities, we'll be working with the sheriff on that," said McMahon.

Mayor Walsh said, "It's called their homes. Go home!"

Deputy Mayor Owens said people need to be careful of the language they use when posting online.
