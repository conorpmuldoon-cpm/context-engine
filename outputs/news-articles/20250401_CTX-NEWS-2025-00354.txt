Tensions boil over handling of Maria Regina property between Syracuse Mayor, councilors

by Mary Kielar

Tue, April 1, 2025 at 3:38 PM
Updated Tue, April 1, 2025 at 6:26 PM

SYRACUSE, N.Y. — Syracuse Mayor Ben Walsh's Office said he stopped communicating with Mark Congel, the property owner of the Maria Regina campus, in 2022 after he became "increasingly unresponsive."

Now, the City of Syracuse Division of Code Enforcement has ordered the demolition of the St. Anthony's Convent on the campus after it was heavily damaged in a massive fire that tore through it on March 21.

Syracuse Common Councilor Marty Nave, whose district contains the old campus, spoke out during a study session last Wednesday as the city's Deputy Commissioner of Code Enforcement and Zoning Administration addressed councilors about the property.

Nave asked why the city hadn't gone after Congel sooner. He said, "That was my worst fear confirmed on Friday. I knew someday this might happen. My constituents are just broken up about this."

"How we let someone like Mr. Congel," said Nave. "We could've gone after him eight years ago or 10 years agoand we didn't. We didn't go after him. We just allowed it to go on and on and on."

Councilor Nave spoke with the I-Team on Tuesday, sharing that Mayor Ben Walsh ended up in the Common Council office after the study session last week. He claimed the Mayor raised his voice at him. He said, "[Walsh] was upset that I asked this question, why didn't I talk to him?"

Nave said he told Walsh that he speaks on behalf of his constituents.

"He was very upset with me because I asked Jake [Dishaw] that simple question, why didn't we go after Congel?" said Nave.

The I-Team obtained an email from Mayor Walsh to Common Council staff later that day.

"I took the opportunity to address a comment he made earlier at the meeting that I disagreed with. It was the wrong place and the wrong time," the email said in part.

Still, Common Council President Pro Tempore Pat Hogan said a formal apology was necessary to Councilor Nave.

"I want to just express my dismay with the actions of our Chief Executive of the city after the study session last week," Hogan said, during the regular meeting on Monday. He added, "I would expect better behavior from the mayor of this city and I would also expect that a letter to Councilor Nave apologizing for his actions."

Since then, both Hogan and City Hall confirmed that Mayor Walsh and Councilor Hogan spoke on the phone about the incident. Hogan said he was taken aback by the Mayor's behavior. He feels a formal apology is still necessary to Councilor Nave, despite the Mayor allegedly telling him he wasn't planning on making one right now.

Walsh and Nave apologized to each other at the opening day of the Syracuse Mets.
