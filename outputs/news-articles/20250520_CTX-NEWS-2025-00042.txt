I-Team: A closer look at what's behind the Syracuse Mayor's budget amendment vetoes

SYRACUSE, N.Y. — The Syracuse Common Council is on the clock, with the task of carefully reviewing Syracuse Mayor Ben Walsh's budget vetoes. They must adopt a final budget by June 2.

The mayor told the I-team that he had put forward 45 vetoes to the council's 82 amendments in a way that prevented cutting costs from constituent services, quality of life, and public safety initiatives.

PREVIOUS COVERAGE | Mayor issues 45 vetoes to Common Council's amendments on Syracuse city budget

It means no property tax increase, which is something the common council nixed from the mayor’s original proposed nearly $350 million budget.

He's also proposed a small hike in water rates for those in the city, which translates to about $5 more a year.

"I objected to all of the amendments related to public safety, police and fire," he said Monday afternoon.

The council wants to cut funding going to police salaries, field services and overtime.

Mayor Walsh said that means 20 positions, 10 crossing guards and 10 special patrol officers would be eliminated.

Plus, he added there's a risk of losing ShotSpotter, which a surveillance system that alerts law enforcement to gunfire. He said it would also risk losing the anonymous tip line platform people use to submit information to police.

It's the same with the Syracuse Fire Department. Walsh highlighted 11 vetoes that he said would hinder dispatch systems and the department's ability to repair and replace equipment.

"If they unanimously, or not, overrule all the vetoes, there will be impacts to services, there will be impacts to jobs and our constituents will see a lower level of service in certain areas that they don't deserve," he said.

Hours after the mayor presented his vetoes, Council President Pro Tempore Pat Hogan said a tax hike is worse than trying to adjust funding to city departments.

"None of my constituents are nervous about that," he said. "What they were nervous about is a tax hike. They're very nervous about it."

Hogan is running for Mayor, as well as his fellow Common Councilor Chol Majok. Deputy Mayor Sharon Owens is also vying for a shot at Walsh's job. All of whom are Democrats.
