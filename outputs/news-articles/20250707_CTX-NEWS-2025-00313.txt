Syracuse, N.Y. — The family of a man who was fatally shot nine years ago by a Syracuse police officer at a chaotic Father’s Day barbecue has requested a new trial in their lawsuit against the
officer.
The family’s lawyer, Fred Lichtmacher, said in court papers filed Tuesday that
the two-week trial
earlier this year was not fair and that the jury’s verdict didn’t make sense. He also claimed some potential jurors who were Black were improperly excluded.
He asked the judge handling the case, Chief U.S. District Judge Brenda Sannes, to order a new trial.
In the original trial,
jurors ruled
after two days of deliberations that the officer, Kelsey Francemone, used excessive force when she fatally shot the man, Gary Porter, after a gunfight that broke out at the barbecue on June 19, 2016, on the Near West Side.
The jurors, however, also appeared to agree with key parts of Francemone’s testimony at trial. They told the judge they believed she did face a man who had a gun and was shooting at a crowded party.
They ultimately awarded $10,000 to Porter’s estate for conscious pain and suffering.
Lichtmacher said the verdict was the result of an “improper compromise.”
“For the jury to have found, correctly, that the defendant used excessive force,” he said, “but incorrectly that the decedent had a gun is entirely inconsistent.”
Some observers have derided the jury’s decisions as confusing and contradictory.
Lichtmacher also said the trial judge erred when she allowed lawyers for Francemone to dismiss most of the Black potential jurors.
Sannes said at the time that there were credible, race-neutral reasons for striking the jurors.
Lichtmacher also continued to question the credibility of the testimony by Francemone and witnesses called in her defense.
Lichtmacher’s arguments in favor of a new trial arrive as Sannes determines whether Francemone is covered by a legal principle called qualified immunity, which would shield her from liability in the case.
The principle, established by the U.S. Supreme Court, protects government officials like police officers from being held responsible in lawsuits for depriving a person of their constitutional rights.
Police officers cannot be held liable in civil cases unless similar actions have already been found unconstitutional in a previous court case.
The two-week civil trial in federal court put the actions of Francemone, who had been with the department for less than two years, under a microscope.
Francemone ran by herself toward gunfire that had erupted at the barbecue at the James Geddes public housing complex.
People at the barbecue began stampeding away from the shots, creating a chaotic scene. Officials said at least 37 shots were fired.
Francemone found three people shooting from near a car in a small parking lot off Tully Street on the southern side of the housing complex.
She
testified
that she had repeatedly identified herself as a police officer and ordered the shooters to drop their guns.
Francemone then fired twice at a man she said she saw turning toward her with a gun at her as he ran.
People attending the barbecue pounced twice on Francemone after she shot Porter, stealing items from her uniform and striking her with a glass bottle.
Witnesses for the plaintiff disputed many aspects of her account.
In conjunction with their excessive force verdict, the jurors also answered three questions, formally known as special interrogatories, about the facts of the case. They agreed with key parts of Francemone’s account.
The jury was asked:
Did Francemone prove that Porter had a gun at some point that night? Yes, the jury said.
Did Francemone prove that she had a reasonable, even if mistaken, belief that Porter was one of several people firing a gun that night? Yes.
Did Francemone prove that she had a reasonable, even if mistaken, belief that Porter had a gun on him before she fired her final shots at him? Yes.
Sol Muñoz, a City Hall spokesperson, said city lawyers would respond to the filing in court papers.
