SYRACUSE, N.Y. — A new fence put up outside the Rescue Mission in Syracuse is drawing a lot of criticism from those who live around it. While the city says it’s a step toward curbing violence and drug activity, many experiencing homelessness say it’s isolating them from the city, from help, and from each other.

The chain-link fence went up this week along Dickerson Street, a troubled area that connects the Rescue Mission to downtown Syracuse. The city, working alongside police and public works crews, says it’s part of a public safety initiative meant to keep drugs and other criminals away from the area’s most vulnerable residents.

But some say the fence feels less like protection and more like punishment.

The Rescue Mission is Syracuse’s largest homeless shelter. Dickerson Street, which runs behind it, has long been a hotspot for drug use, overdoses, and fights. Residents and city officials alike say the block has seen decades of problems. Many are worsened by the area’s ongoing housing and mental health crises.

Still, those living near the new barrier say the fence isn’t fixing the root issues and in some cases, it’s making things worse.

“They caging people like animals. They treating the streets like prison,” said Tryail Martin, who’s currently unhoused.

Critics also say the fence could delay emergency response times. Dickerson Street is now a dead-end, meaning ambulances have to make tight three-point turns to exit the area after responding to overdoses or other calls.

“They have to go in and make a U-turn just to come through and save someone,” Monahan said. “It takes away that much quality time from medical personnel we actually need.”

Beyond safety, residents say the fence is cutting off access to family, rideshares, and basic human connections.

“I got an uncle over there and I can’t even come say hi no more,” Martin said. “Since they put this fence up, I haven’t seen my uncle or my aunt.”

Others say the fence hasn’t stopped drug activity—it’s just pushed it farther down the block.

“It didn’t change anything, for real for real,” said a woman who lives at the Rescue Mission and asked to remain anonymous. “It just made things go on the other side.”
