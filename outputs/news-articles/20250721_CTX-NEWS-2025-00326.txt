To the Editor:
In response to Onondaga County Executive Ryan McMahon’s claim that an aquarium is the
“perfect use” for Onondaga Lake cleanup funds,
I must strongly disagree. The most effective use of these funds is to continue the essential work of cleaning up and restoring the lake itself, rather than diverting public money into over-budget development projects, such as the Inner Harbor aquarium.

The Honeywell funds McMahon is now raiding were meant to restore natural areas damaged by years of pollution, including wetlands, fishing sites and trails around the lake. Instead, McMahon is using $2.5 million from this fund to patch up a gap in aquarium construction costs. That’s not environmental restoration — it’s fiscal sleight of hand.
Meanwhile, actual cleanup work is far from complete. In June,
Onondaga County was fined
for letting sewage back up in parking lots and Onondaga Lake. Just last week, we learned that the county’s new $23 million biosolids sewage facility is
so toxic inside
that workers refuse to enter it. If they can’t safely manage sewage now, how can we pretend the lake is ready for luxury development?
Onondaga Lake should be supporting the region by providing a nourishing environment for the natural world and all living beings (including humans). The lake should be a source of safe drinking water. The truth is that the lake still faces ongoing threats, including discharges from sewage systems and industrial waste. Communities near Onondaga Lake deserve clean water, not grand distractions. And the Onondaga Nation deserves the return of Maple Bay, where ceremonies and stewardship can help heal this sacred site. The time to listen to and learn from the original inhabitants and ceremonial caretakers of the lake is now.
Imagine an Onondaga Lake where fish thrive, children swim safely, and the Onondaga Nation leads in ecological restoration. That’s a real future — one built on repair, not cover-ups. If we want economic development, it must begin with a healthy environment. A clean lake benefits everyone. A fish tank benefits a few. It is time for the county to act on its
2011
and
2016
promises and return a piece of Onondaga Lake, such as Maple Bay, to the Onondaga Nation.
Let’s clean the lake — and return Maple Bay.
Betty Hill (Lyons)
Executive Director
American Indian Law Alliance
