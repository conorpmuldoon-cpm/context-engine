Syracuse school zone traffic warnings drop 41% as enforcement program progresses

The City of Syracuse has reported a significant decrease in traffic warnings as its new School Zone Traffic Enforcement program approaches the midpoint of its grace period. Between Sept. 16 and Sept. 30, automated speed and red-light cameras detected approximately 35,300 warnings, a 41.2% drop from the 60,000 recorded in the program's first two weeks.

The data reveals that about 34,700 of these warnings were for speeding, while 600 were for running red lights. Notably, the highest recorded speed was 64 mph in a 20-mph zone, occurring twice near Dr. Weeks Elementary School and PSLA at Fowler. Additionally, 21.6% of warnings were for speeds over 15 mph above the limit, and 47% of drivers received multiple warnings. "The system is working," said Syracuse Police Chief Cecile, noting that drivers are heeding the warnings and adjusting their speeds. "We're not asking a lot. Just give the kids one less thing to worry about," he continued.

One of the speed cameras in Syracuse. (Photo by CNY Central)

Mayor Walsh also commented on the findings, "These numbers show that automated enforcement is already making a difference. We are seeing measurable progress in just a few weeks. But the data also tells us that far too many drivers are still running red lights and exceeding school zone speed limits ... "

... We need every driver to take this seriously. Slowing down and abiding by red lights saves lives.

The city has implemented a 60-day grace period, double the industry standard, to raise awareness and promote safe driving habits. Full enforcement, including $50 fines for violations, will begin Nov. 3, according to Syracuse Police. A $25 late penalty will apply to unpaid fines, and citations will be mailed to vehicle owners.

In addition to the speed cameras, the School Bus Stop-Arm Camera Program, active since April 3, has recorded 2,000 violations, underscoring the need for continued driver awareness around school buses. The city urges drivers to slow down and stay alert to protect students and families traveling to and from school.
