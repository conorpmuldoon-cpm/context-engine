SYRACUSE, N.Y. — Increased State Police patrols and a new coordinated public awareness campaign launched this week with the goal of stopping truck drivers from crashing into the Onondaga Lake Parkway bridge and other low bridges across New York State.

“Check Your Height, Know It’s Right,” originated at the New York State Department of Transportation and is now being adopted by 16 states and Washington, D.C.

In one of the ads, actors portray friends who rent a truck for a move. "You think we'll make it," one of them says. Next thing, the truck hits a bridge. "Next time she can hire professional movers," one of the actors says sitting in the back of the disabled vehicle. The commercial ends with the other woman tossing her belongings on the ground and the woman who was driving the truck giving a funny exclamation. Another video in the series references personal GPS systems which don't typically warn of low bridges. The driver crashes into a bridge, hangs his head while the voice on the GPS exclaims," Calculating!"

The videos use light humor to drive home an issue that has been deadly serious in Central New York. No crash into the Parkway bridge came with more dire consequences than the 2010 Megabus disaster. The double decker bus hit the bridge killing 4 people. The driver has been taking directions from his personal GPS.

Officials say in 2024 there were 350 bridge strikes in New York State, usually involving commercial and large truck operators failing to recognize their vehicle’s height or warning signs and resulting in collisions with bridges and overpasses.

Light comedy and a catch phrase to help stop Parkway bridge strikes?

As part of the enforcement arm of the campaign, State Troopers will focus active patrols in areas where there have been bridge strikes by large commercial vehicles - including the Onondaga Lake Parkway.

The Parkway truss bridge that carries trains was built in 1971. After a CNYCentral Town Hall Meeting on the problem, New York State took new action. The DOT closed the ramp from I-81 to the Parkway. It added new warning signs and narrowed the driving lanes approaching the bridge in both directions. And still it happens.New York State is hoping the "Check Your Height, Know It's Right" campaign will help.
