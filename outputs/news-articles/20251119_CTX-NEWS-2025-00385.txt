ICE comments on Syracuse business owner’s self-deportation amid visa controversy

by Charlotte Hansen

Tue, November 18, 2025 at 10:18 PM
Updated Thu, November 20, 2025 at 9:16 AM
A quiet Tuesday morning in Syracuse’s Hawley-Green neighborhood turned into a tense standoff this week, as immigration agents tried to detain a local business owner, and neighbors scrambled to help him leave the country on his own terms.

LIVERPOOL, N.Y. — Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) officials responded to CNY Central's request for comment on the case of a Syracuse business owner who self-deported to avoid capture by ICE agents.

Jeremy Dottin-Reina, a French citizen living in the U.S. on an expired visa, was approached by unmarked cars on Nov. 11. They followed him from his Liverpool home, to the business he owns with his husband Rick Reina. The business is Syracuse Soapworks on Hawley Avenue.

RELATED | Community rallies to help Syracuse business owner leave U.S. after ICE detention attempt

With the help of community members, Jeremy was able to evade ICE and self-deported to his home country.

In a statement, an ICE spokesman said that Dottin was “an illegal alien from France whose circumstances were entirely avoidable.”

The agency alleges it was not Dottin's husband, but a female fiancé, who applied for the visa that authorized his original admission to the U.S. They also mentioned that Dottin's violation of immigration law caused problems among the community.

“By choosing to overstay his K-1 visa, Dottin placed himself in violation of immigration law, which led to enforcement actions and contributed to unwarranted fear within the community.” ICE officials said.

ICE also added that Homeland Security's app has a tool to facilitate compliance with the immigration process, which the agency says could have been used to address this specific situation in a lawful and efficient manner. "This resource was available to Mr. Dottin and could have been utilized to address his situation in a lawful and efficient manner. Not only would he have been given a free flight home, but he would have also received a $1000 stipend and the chance to come back to the U.S. the legal way," an ICE spokesperson said.
