By
Jeremy Boyer | JBoyer@syracuse.com
Syracuse, N.Y. ― A multi-year effort to revitalize
Syracuse’s South Avenue corridor
has picked up some momentum as two housing projects are taking key steps forward.
The projects would add a combined 126 apartments, one of the largest additions of new housing in the city’s Southwest area in years if not decades. And they would offer average rents that are below market rate, a key opportunity for a neighborhood with significant economic disadvantages.
A four-story building across from the Price Rite grocery store would include 51 units priced at incomes ranging from 30% to 80% of the area’s median income, with sizes ranging from studios to three bedrooms. In the Syracuse metropolitan area, those income restrictions translate into a one-person household earning between $22,000 and $58,000 per year.
Less than a mile to the south, new four-story buildings on opposite sides of South Avenue would provide mostly one-bedroom affordable apartments for senior citizens, with ground-floor restaurant, retail and event spaces. Rents would target seniors earning between 30% and 70% of the median income for the area.
Developers behind the projects are applying this week for financing through the state’s
Low Income Housing Tax Credit Program
.
Both projects are seeking Syracuse Common Council approval for property tax relief. The council’s Economic Development Committee will discuss those requests at 10 a.m. Wednesday. The project sites are all vacant parcels currently owned by the Greater Syracuse Land Bank and have not been generating any tax revenue for several years.
Jubilee Homes, a Syracuse community development nonprofit that has been
working
to bring new housing and businesses to the
financially struggling
Southwest area, is partnering with Massachusetts-based BLD Properties for the $28 million project across from Price Rite.
BLD is a new firm started by Kenan Bigby, who was a managing director for more than 20 years at Boston-based Trinity Financial. After meeting Jubilee Homes’ Executive Director Walt Dixie at a conference, Bigby started the project more than four years ago when he was with Trinity.
As he was preparing to leave Trinity, he negotiated to keep the Syracuse project in his portfolio.
“It was a little bit on the smaller side for Trinity, and so it never really rose to the top of the pipeline, but this was a project that I believed in and wanted to see happen,” he said. “The size actually works well for being one of the early projects in this new venture. So everything sort of fell into place.”
Bigby said proximity to the Price Rite, which Jubilee
helped open
in 2017, makes the site attractive.
“I just saw there was the opportunity to sort of build on an earlier success, and the idea of doing multifamily across the street from a grocery store is appealing,” he said. “It’s just a nice amenity to have when you’re thinking about locations for a project like that.”
It also helped to have a cheap, shovel-ready site available. The land bank has agreed to sell the eight parcels that make up the project footprint for $30,000.
In addition to applying for the state’s tax credits and property tax relief from the city, BLD is working through the city’s zoning process this month. On Wednesday, the Board of Zoning Appeals approved a variance for the project allowing it to exceed the maximum building length because of limitations on the site. That decision cleared the way for the city Planning Commission to complete its review. On Monday, the commission approved the project site plan.
Under the proposal before the common council, annual tax payments would start at $26,775 and increase by 2% each year for 15 years. The project is also receiving a $1 million state grant via the
Downtown Revitalization Initiative
program that awarded almost $10 million for projects in the area in 2022.
Bigby is aiming for construction to start by the middle of next year, with completion by the end of 2027.
A separate project a few blocks south, near South Avenue’s intersection with West Colvin Street, is also experiencing renewed momentum. The Women’s Economic Institute, founded by Syracuse resident
Charlene Tarver
, has been looking to develop a senior housing project with ground-floor commercial space for several years.
Tarver’s $42 million Southside Renaissance Project picked up a key partner last year when Brooklyn-based
St. Nicks Alliance
agreed to become a co-developer.
In the spring of 2024, the Greater Syracuse Land Bank board declined to extend a contract to sell eight vacant land parcels after a previous partner working with Tarver had failed to submit a complete low-income tax credit application with the state. The board encouraged her to find a co-developer to help build out the project team and come back. That’s what she did, and in January 2025, the board agreed to sell the land for $209,000.
Another key partner in the project is the
New York Local Initiatives Support Corp
., through its New York Land Opportunity Program. That program assists organizations with resource gathering to develop affordable housing.
“The Women’s Economic Institute is painfully aware of the pervasive poverty on the City’s Southside,” Tarver said. “Stakeholders like St. Nicks Alliance and LISC NY have both a demonstrated commitment to building affordable housing and a longstanding record in neighborhood and economic revitalization. It was important to the WEI that we partner with a master developer who understands the impact of food insecurity on Black health. St. Nicks gets it and is committed to building housing that is designed around food, health and economic equity. That’s how you eliminate poverty.”
The commercial space planned for the project includes a food hall, retail incubator, day care center and workforce development programming.
The Southside Renaissance Project’s proposed 15-year property tax agreement with the city would start with an annual payment of $39,375, with a 2% yearly increase.
The development team will hold a land dedication ceremony at 11 a.m. Friday at the project site on South Avenue.
